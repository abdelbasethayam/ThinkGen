Each question carries a weight (1â€“4). Later questions in a session are worth more. Each task â€” opening question, answer evaluation, hint generation, clarification, gap teaching, topic splitting, final report â€” is handled by a dedicated prompt with carefully constrained output format (JSON for evaluations, plain text for hints). The Moments That Made It Work

Some things about ThinkGen surprised us during testing. Not bugs â€” moments where the system did something unexpectedly good.

The pivot rule. If a student scores â‰¤ 2 for three questions in a row, the prompt tells the model to change angle completely â€” switch question type, try a different analogy, come at the concept from a different direction. We tested this on Diffusion Models (notoriously hard for beginners) and watched the conversation transform from frustrating to illuminating when the model switched from "explain the math" to "imagine you're trying to reconstruct a shredded photo one piece at a time."

The gibberish detector. A naive system accepts anything. If a student types "asdfghjkl" or "idk" they'd get scored and move on, wasting their time and corrupting the mastery calculation. We built a simple regex check that catches low-information-density inputs before they even hit the API. Score 1 is applied automatically, and the student is prompted to try again.

The Strictness Problem Our biggest tuning challenge was preventing the model from being too generous. LLMs naturally want to be encouraging. Left unconstrained, LLaMA would give 4/5 to answers that were vague-but-confident. We added an explicit warning: "If you give score 4 or 5 to a wrong answer, you fail your job." Harsh â€” but it worked.

What We Actually Learned Building This

The irony of building an AI that surfaces learning gaps is that the build process surfaced ours. A few hard-won lessons:

ğŸ§ 
Prompt engineering is product design. The quality of ThinkGen's questions, tone, and adaptability is entirely a function of the prompts. We rewrote the evaluation prompt 15+ times. Every word choice matters. Saying "you fail your job" to the model for bad scoring sounds absurd but measurably improved strictness.
ğŸ”„
State is everything in adaptive systems. Streamlit's session state was the glue holding the experience together. Without carefully tracking scores, questions, answers, and clarifications, the model would have no memory and the "adaptive" part would be impossible.
âš–ï¸
Strictness vs. warmth is a real design tension. Too strict = students feel crushed and stop engaging. Too lenient = false confidence. We landed on strict scoring with warm, empathetic feedback text â€” the number is honest, the words are encouraging.

Constraints drive creativity. No dataset, no training, no paid APIs beyond a free tier â€” these constraints forced us to think about what language models can do purely from prompting. The answer, it turns out, is a lot.
